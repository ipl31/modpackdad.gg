Afro-Cuban Fire

Afro-Cuban ~90 min + backup High & Steady Descarga · Rumba · Latin Jazz

A high, steady, percussion-first run through the Afro-Cuban tradition — Nuyorican descargas, sacred rumba and batá roots, Havana fire and West-Coast Latin jazz. Hidden gems up front, big names earning their place. The thread throughout: clave, congas, and the spirit that travels with them.

Main Set ~90 min
01

Grupo Folklórico y Experimental Nuevayorquino — Cinco en uno callejero

Lo Dice Todo · 1976

The Nuyorican avant-garde's manifesto: a loose collective convened by percussionists Andy and Jerry González and producer René López to strip salsa back to its Afro-Cuban and Afro-Puerto Rican roots, right as Fania was polishing it for the dance floor. The title means "five in one street-style" — five rhythms (bomba, mozambique, batucada, calypso, rumba) welded into one suite, with Heny Álvarez leading the vocals over a who's-who lineup including Chocolate Armenteros and Manny Oquendo. One of the most fiercely authentic Latin records ever cut in New York.

02

Ray Barretto — Cocinando

Que Viva La Música · 1972

Barretto, the Brooklyn-born "King of the Hard Hands," was the conguero who'd already played countless jazz dates before becoming one of Fania's defining bandleaders. "Cocinando" ("Cooking") is a hypnotic, slow-burn descarga that lets the rhythm section stretch out — famously the cut that opened Leon Gast's 1972 documentary Our Latin Thing, the film that introduced the world to the New York salsa explosion. Adalberto Santiago on lead vocals, Orestes Vilató on timbales: untouchable groove.

03

Típica 73 — La Candela

La Candela · 1975

Típica 73 began as a breakaway crew of Barretto sidemen and became one of the most adventurous bands of the era — eventually traveling to Havana to record with Cuban musicians at a time when that was nearly unthinkable. La Candela is widely cited as their finest hour: Adalberto Santiago up front, Sonny Bravo on piano, Nelson González on tres, Orestes Vilató on timbales. Their calling card was a modernized take on the Cuban típica conjunto sound — hot, sophisticated, rhythmically fearless.

04

Manny Oquendo's Conjunto Libre — Que Humanidad

Ritmo, Sonido y Estilo · 1983

After leaving Eddie Palmieri's La Perfecta, timbalero/bongocero Manny Oquendo co-founded Libre with bassist Andy González to keep the trombone-and-percussion tradition uncompromised — the band became a "finishing school" for Latin jazz musicians. The title nods to their core values, and the music is a clinic in the crescendo: a battery of trombones that can slide from a tender son montuno into a roaring mambo and end in a full descarga. Relentless collective heat.

05

Jerry González — Agüeybaná Zemí

Ya Yo Me Curé · 1980

Trumpeter/conguero Jerry González's debut as a leader, recorded in Willow, NY in 1979 with Kip Hanrahan — the seed of what became the Fort Apache Band, though the group wasn't yet named. The opener was written by vocalist Frankie Rodríguez and named for the Taíno cacique who first received Ponce de León; its call-and-response vocals plant the whole record in Afro-Caribbean identity. (The "Fort Apache" name was later adopted as a defiant rebuke to the film's racist depiction of the Bronx.) A foundational document of the Bronx Latin-jazz vanguard.

06

Eddie Palmieri — Vámonos Pa'l Monte

Vámonos Pa'l Monte · 1971

A cornerstone of both the salsa and Latin-jazz canons, from the pianist whose percussive, McCoy Tyner–influenced left hand rewired what the instrument could do in this music. The title track is built around an electrifying keyboard dialogue between Eddie on electric piano and his older brother Charlie Palmieri on organ — two of the great Nuyorican pianists trading lightning, with Ronnie Cuber and Chocolate Armenteros in the cast. "Let's head for the hills" carries a coded, roots-seeking resonance that runs deep through Afro-Cuban tradition.

07

Cachao — Descarga Cubana

Cuban Jam Session "Descargas" · 1957

Israel "Cachao" López, with his brother Orestes, helped invent the mambo in the late-1930s charanga Arcaño y sus Maravillas — and then, with these sessions, effectively codified the descarga (the Cuban jam) as a recorded art form. The album was famously cut at Panart's Havana studio in a single five-hour session starting around 4 a.m., after the musicians finished their nightclub gigs. Tight, miniature improvisations rather than sprawling jams — a genuine milestone, later inducted into the U.S. National Recording Registry.

▶ YouTube◆ Buy (lossless)
08

Mongo Santamaría — Mi Guaguancó

Yambú · 1958

After leaving Cuba and anchoring Cal Tjader's group, conguero Mongo Santamaría made Yambú as a deliberate deep-dive into authentic Afro-Cuban folklore — yambú, columbia and guaguancó, the three pillars of rumba. "Mi Guaguancó" sits squarely in that tradition: layered hand drums, clave and call-and-response chant rather than jazz arrangement, with an extraordinary cast that includes Willie Bobo and Al McKibbon. The track was dropped from some later CD reissues, which makes the original LP the one to seek out.

09

Irakere — Bacalao con Pan

Teatro Amadeo Roldán Recital · 1974

Led by pianist Chucho Valdés (son of Bebo), Irakere detonated Cuban music in the 1970s by fusing jazz, rock, funk and the sacred batá-drum tradition — the name itself is a Yoruba-derived word evoking the forest. "Bacalao con Pan" ("codfish and bread") is the dancefloor bomb: wah-wah guitar, ferocious horns and a riot of percussion — early Cuban jazz-funk at full tilt. The personnel pool included future giants Paquito D'Rivera and Arturo Sandoval; a holy grail for crate-diggers.

▶ YouTube◆ Buy (lossless)
10

Patato y Totico — Ya Yo E

Patato & Totico · 1967

A rare, jewel-like meeting of masters: conguero Carlos "Patato" Valdés (a key figure in the tunable conga drum) and rumbero/vocalist Eugenio "Totico" Arango, joined by Cachao on bass and the blind tres genius Arsenio Rodríguez — a living bridge to the deepest roots of Cuban son and rumba. The album is essentially pure guaguancó: voices, hand drums, clave and tres, with almost no concession to commercial salsa. The sound of a Havana tenement courtyard transplanted to New York.

11

Chocolate Armenteros — Chocolate Aquí

Chocolate Aquí · 1974

Alfredo "Chocolate" Armenteros was the trumpeter whose burnished, vibrato-rich tone became the definitive trompeta cubana sound — a veteran of Arsenio Rodríguez's conjunto who graced hundreds of New York salsa dates. Chocolate Aquí marked his belated debut as leader of his own conjunto, produced by Louie Ramírez, with Libre's Manny Oquendo and Andy González among the guests. His playing here is the template every salsa trumpeter since has chased.

▶ YouTube◆ Buy (lossless)
12

Pello el Afrokán — María Caracoles

Single · ~1964 · ritmo Mozambique

Pedro Izquierdo, "Pello el Afrokán," created one of post-revolutionary Cuba's most explosive rhythms: the mozambique (1963), a carnival-derived stew blending Abakuá, Yoruba, Congo and Carabalí elements (and, despite the name, nothing to do with the African country). "María Caracoles" is his signature anthem — a thundering wall of drums and chant. A measure of its reach: Santana covered it on their 1977 album Festival. Pure street-carnival energy, massed percussion front and center.

13

Francisco Aguabella — Hitting Hard

Hitting Hard · 1977 (CuBop reissue 1993)

Aguabella was a Matanzas-born master drummer and an olú batá — an initiated keeper of the sacred batá drums of Santería — who carried that lineage from Cuba to American stages with Tito Puente, Eddie Palmieri and Dizzy Gillespie. Hitting Hard is the wilder, secular flip side of his art: a street-style rare-groove fusion of salsa, Latin jazz and funk, later rescued by San Francisco's CuBop label, with the great Cuban conguero Armando Peraza guesting. Sacred tradition meets dancefloor funk.

▶ YouTube◆ Buy (lossless)
14

Cándido — Jingo

Dancin' & Prancin' · 1979

Cándido Camero was a true pioneer — among the first to play multiple congas at once and to fully integrate the instrument into American jazz, across a career stretching from 1940s Havana into the 21st century. By 1979 the elder statesman went disco: "Jingo" is a full-throttle Latin-disco firestorm descended from Nigerian drummer Babatunde Olatunji's "Jingo-Lo-Ba" (1959), by way of Santana. A massive club anthem and a perennial floor-filler.

▶ YouTube◆ Buy (lossless)
15

Sabu Martínez — Asabache

Palo Congo · 1957 · Blue Note

A startling thing to find in the Blue Note catalog: an unapologetically Afro-Cuban ritual record. Conguero Sabú Martínez cut this debut on April 27, 1957, with the blind son master Arsenio Rodríguez and his brothers in the octet. "Asabache" is essentially one long, building conga tirade — drums and chant rooted in Congo-derived palo tradition. A fragment of Afro-Cuban ritual smuggled onto a famous jazz label; it later earned a place in 1001 Albums You Must Hear Before You Die. A fitting close to the main set.

16

Manny Oquendo's Libre — Mejor Que Nunca

Mejor Que Nunca (Better Than Ever) · 1994

Two decades in, Libre proved the title literally — often called their finest latter-day record, released on Milestone and directed by Andy González around Manny Oquendo's distinctive timbal/bongo attack. The trademark four-trombone front line still carries the music from son montuno and guaracha into ferocious mambo and descarga. The sound of a band that spent twenty years refining one idea — collective swing built to a boil — and nailing it.

▶ YouTube◆ Buy (lossless)
17

Joe Cuba Sextet — El Pito (I'll Never Go Back to Georgia)

Single · 1965

The quintessential Latin boogaloo crossover, co-written by Joe Cuba and timbalero/vocalist Jimmy Sabater, with Cheo Feliciano and Sabater singing. The irony: the English hook "I'll never go back to Georgia" was lifted straight from the intro chant of Dizzy Gillespie's "Manteca" — and none of the band had ever been to Georgia; Sabater just loved how the cadence locked into clave. The whistle became the gimmick. A rare record that united Latin and Black audiences at exactly the right moment.

▶ YouTube◆ Buy (lossless)
18

Pucho & His Latin Soul Brothers — Heat!

Heat! · 1968

Henry "Pucho" Brown led one of the great boogaloo/soul-jazz outfits, and Heat! is a prime slab of greasy Latin soul-jazz — Pucho on timbales, Willie Bivens' shimmering vibes, the Pazant brothers' horns and Neal Creque's organ arrangements. Originals and danced-up covers alike, exactly the kind of crate-dig that found a second life with later acid-jazz and hip-hop crowds. Funky, infectious, built for the floor.

19

Tata Güines — Pasaporte

Pasaporte · 1994

Tata Güines (1930–2008) was revered in Cuba simply as one of the greatest congueros who ever lived — a master of touch and tone central to the modern Afro-Cuban descarga. Pasaporte pairs the elder master with the young phenom Miguel "Angá" Díaz (later of Buena Vista and Irakere fame), a generational summit steeped in descarga and rumba, with guest spots from folkloric vocalist Merceditas Valdés. Two virtuosos, decades apart, in conversation through the drum.

▶ YouTube◆ Buy (lossless)
20

Cal Tjader — Cuban Fantasy

Cuban Fantasy · live, 1977

Vibraphonist Cal Tjader was the rare non-Latino who became a genuinely central figure in Latin jazz, building a West Coast sound that carried Afro-Cuban rhythm to a wide American audience. "Cuban Fantasy" is a Ray Bryant composition Tjader revisited over decades; the best-known album bearing the title is a 1977 Great American Music Hall live date with Clare Fischer and Poncho Sanchez. A breezy, cross-rhythmic palate-cleanser deep in the night.

▶ YouTube◆ Buy (lossless)
21

Willie Bobo — Spanish Grease

Spanish Grease · 1965

Timbalero/drummer Willie Bobo (born William Correa) came up under Mongo and Tito Puente before launching his own crossover sound. The title track — the only original on the album, co-written with Melvin Lastie — is the irresistible mid-tempo Latin-soul groove that became his calling card, all relaxed swagger and shaker-and-vibes cool. Recorded at Rudy Van Gelder's studio, it's a perfect distillation of the "Latin grease" aesthetic: not flashy, just deeply in the pocket.

▶ YouTube◆ Buy (lossless)
22

Los Papines — Los Barrios (Rumba)

Cuban rumba

Los Papines are a Havana rumba institution, built from the Abreu family of Marianao — a clan of percussionists who professionalized in the early 1960s and took the name in 1963. Alongside Los Muñequitos de Matanzas, they carried pure, unaccompanied Cuban rumba — guaguancó, columbia, yambú — to stages across the world. Their art is voices, claves, and the conversational interplay of quinto, tres dos and tumbador: rumba in its most distilled, electrifying form.

23

Mongo Santamaría — Afro Blue

Mongo · 1959

The original — Mongo's composition "Afro Blue," recorded for his own debut leader album in May 1959, built on a hypnotic 3-against-2 cross-rhythm. Often called the first jazz standard founded on a genuine African cross-rhythm, it went on to be immortalized by John Coltrane and countless others (Oscar Brown Jr. added the famous lyric the same year). A cornerstone of the entire Afro-Cuban-jazz idiom — and a perfect, circling note to end on.
